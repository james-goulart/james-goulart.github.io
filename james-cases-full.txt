Case Name:
Inverted Loan Request UX

Case Narrative:
From Bank Logic to User Logic: Redesigning a Loan Request Flow That Increased Conversion from <10% to ~80%

Short summary

At Nexus, a peer-to-peer lending marketplace connecting SME borrowers with retail investors, I worked on redesigning the company loan request flow. The original process followed a bank-like model: before a business could even be evaluated, it had to provide a heavy set of supporting documents for credit analysis. This created major friction and kept request conversion below 10%. I proposed inverting the logic: instead of demanding proof upfront, we would trust applicants’ declared information, enrich it with third-party data sources, and only request documents after conditional approval and borrower acceptance. This shift dramatically increased conversion to roughly 80% and created a much more user-centered alternative to the traditional banking experience.

Context

Nexus operated as a peer-to-peer lending marketplace. On one side were small and medium-sized businesses looking for loans, often because traditional banks either under-served them or offered terms that were too expensive. On the other side were retail investors who collectively funded those loans as an investment product.

The loan request flow sat at a critical point in the marketplace: if businesses dropped before completing an application, the platform lost qualified demand before risk assessment even began. That made the application experience not only a UX problem, but also a core business problem.

The problem

The original application process mirrored the logic used by banks. Businesses were asked to provide a large volume of documents early in the journey, before there was any indication that a loan would actually be approved.

This created several problems:

High effort upfront: SME owners had to gather and submit multiple documents before receiving any signal that the process was worth their time.
Low motivation: Because Nexus was not a traditional bank brand, borrowers had less reason to tolerate heavy administrative burden at the start.
Poor conversion: Loan request conversion was below 10%.
Misaligned sequencing: The process optimized for institutional certainty too early, instead of for user momentum.

The standard logic in financial services was clear: ask for documents first, analyze later. But that logic had been inherited from banks rather than designed around the actual needs and behavior of our users.

Key insight

The breakthrough was recognizing that banks use upfront documents for two different purposes at once:

to gather data for credit analysis
to filter for seriousness, intent, and compliance

Those two jobs did not necessarily need to happen at the same moment.

I proposed separating them.

Instead of requiring proof before any value was returned to the borrower, we could:

collect easy-to-provide information first
enrich and validate risk signals through external data sources
make a preliminary decision
only then request documentary proof as part of final compliance and verification

That would reduce friction without abandoning control.

My role

I proposed the change in application logic and helped shape the new experience around a user-first sequence rather than a bank-first sequence. The work required aligning product thinking with credit, compliance, and data realities, since the challenge was not just simplifying an interface but redesigning the logic of how risk evaluation entered the journey.

Constraints

This was not a simple form simplification project. We had to work within real operational constraints:

financial risk could not be ignored
compliance still required proof of revenue, cash flow, and business legitimacy
borrowers needed to authorize checks on credit history and related records
the company had lower default trust than incumbent banks
the process still had to protect investors on the other side of the marketplace

The solution therefore had to reduce user burden without weakening risk controls.

The solution

We redesigned the loan request flow around progressive commitment.

1. We removed document upload from the start of the application

Instead of asking businesses to gather a large set of files upfront, we began with information they could provide quickly and easily.

2. We asked for structured self-reported business data

Applicants entered information such as company identity, ownership, and starting revenue, along with other details needed to assess the request.

3. We used external data providers to enrich the application

Using the company tax ID and the tax IDs of associated owners and stakeholders, we mapped relevant entities and pulled available signals from external sources.

4. We requested permission to access formal credit records

Applicants authorized checks tied to central banking and credit history systems, allowing us to evaluate risk without requiring immediate document submission.

5. We moved proof documents to the post-approval stage

Only after a loan was conditionally approved—and the borrower chose to proceed—did we ask for documentary evidence to verify reported revenue, cash flow, and compliance details.

In other words, we moved from “prove first, maybe receive value later” to “receive a serious evaluation first, then verify before completion.”

Outcome

The change produced a major increase in conversion.

Loan request conversion increased from under 10% to roughly 80%
Positive progression into credit analysis rose to over 50%, based on my recollection
Some applicants were still rejected later, either because their declared information did not support approval or because documentation failed final verification
Even with those downstream losses, the overall journey performed far better than the original bank-like model

The result was not just a more efficient funnel. It was a better-designed business experience that matched the expectations and constraints of SME owners.

Why this worked

This redesign worked because it attacked the real source of friction: premature burden.

SME borrowers were willing to provide information, but asking them to compile proof documents before they knew whether the opportunity was real imposed too much cost too early. By changing the sequence, we preserved trust and momentum.

It also reinforced the brand promise. Instead of behaving like a smaller copy of a bank, Nexus behaved like a different kind of financial service—faster, more modern, and more aligned with the user’s reality.

Most importantly, the project showed that in regulated products, innovation does not always come from removing controls. Sometimes it comes from placing controls at the right moment in the journey.

What I learned

This case taught me that some of the strongest product opportunities come from questioning process assumptions that everyone treats as fixed.

In this case, the opportunity was not just “make the form easier.” It was to ask a deeper question:

Why are we asking for proof at this stage at all?

Once that assumption was challenged, we were able to redesign the experience around user commitment, not institutional habit. The result improved conversion, preserved necessary safeguards, and helped define a more differentiated product experience.

Case Name:
Investor Wallet

Case Narrative:
Fixing the Last-Mile of Investing: How a Wallet Simplified Funding, Payouts, and Reinvestment

Short summary

At Nexoos, I worked on an operationally painful part of the investor journey in our peer-to-peer lending marketplace. Each week, investors could reserve allocations in newly approved company loans, but they still needed to manually transfer funds afterward to complete the investment. Many users either delayed the transfer or failed to complete it at all, creating operational complexity, broken expectations, and a poor investing experience. Our first solution was to create waiting lists, but their efficacy was limited since the inestors in line had increasingly less time to transfer. To fundamentally solve this, we introduced a wallet model through a fintech partner: investors would pre-fund their account, and when they clicked to invest, the money would be debited automatically. Payouts from companies were also returned to the same wallet, where balances earned interest and could be easily reinvested. The result was a much smoother experience for users, a significant reduction in operational friction, and a stronger foundation for repeat investing.

Context

Nexoos was a peer-to-peer lending marketplace connecting companies in need of financing with retail investors looking for yield opportunities.

On the investor side, the marketplace operated on a scheduled cadence. Each week, after company loans had passed credit analysis, a new batch of investment opportunities would open for investors. Users could choose where to allocate their money, effectively reserving participation in those loans.

But there was a disconnect in the process: selecting an investment did not mean the investment was actually funded. After making their choice on the platform, investors still had to transfer the money manually to our financial institution partner. Only then could the investment be confirmed.

This created a fragile experience at exactly the point where certainty mattered most.

The problem

The original investing flow had a serious execution gap between intent and completion.

Users would browse opportunities and decide to invest, but the process was not finished inside the product. Instead, they had to perform a separate financial transfer afterward, often under time pressure.

That led to several problems:

Drop-off after commitment: some investors selected opportunities but never completed the transfer
Late payments: some users transferred after the deadline or too close to it
Operational complexity: the team had to manage waiting lists, reallocate capacity, and manually follow up with investors
Unreliable fulfillment: reservations were not always backed by actual cash
Poor user experience: investing felt more cumbersome than it should have, especially for repeat users

In practice, the system treated an “investment click” as if it were a confirmed action, when in reality it was only a provisional intent that still depended on an external manual step.

That mismatch created friction for both users and the business.

Key insight

The key insight was that the problem was not just about reminders or better transfer instructions. The real problem was architectural.

We had designed the investment journey in two disconnected stages:

decision inside the platform
payment outside the platform

That separation created uncertainty and operational burden.

The solution was to bring the act of investing and the act of funding much closer together.

A wallet model solved this by introducing pre-funded liquidity. If investors placed money in advance into a wallet account, then the actual investment action could become immediate and reliable. That would eliminate the gap between “I want to invest” and “the investment is funded.”

A second insight was even more valuable strategically: once money flowed back from repayments into the same wallet, reinvestment would become dramatically easier. The wallet was not just a payment fix. It was an infrastructure layer for better investor behavior over time.

My role

I worked on this case from both a product and operational perspective. I had also managed the operations team in the past, which gave me direct visibility into the downstream complexity caused by failed or delayed transfers.

That mattered because the issue was not only visible in the interface. It was visible in the daily operational pain behind the interface: waiting lists, manual follow-ups, reallocations, and uncertainty around whether investment demand would actually materialize.

I helped drive the move toward a wallet-based solution by aligning user experience needs with operational realities and by shaping the case for a new partner-based financial model.

Constraints

This was not a simple UI optimization. There were meaningful business and operational constraints:

investments opened on a time-based weekly cycle
allocation needed to be reliable once investors selected an opportunity
the solution had to work with a regulated financial partner
users needed to trust leaving funds inside an account connected to the platform
money also needed to flow back cleanly when borrower installments were repaid
the model had to reduce manual work without making the investment experience feel more complicated upfront

There was also a trade-off to manage: asking people to fund a wallet before investing introduces an extra preparatory step. For the solution to work, that step had to feel worthwhile and intuitive.

Solution

We redesigned the investor flow around a wallet account provided through a fintech partner.

1. We introduced pre-funded investor accounts

Instead of selecting an investment first and transferring money later, users would add funds to a wallet account in advance.

2. We connected investing directly to wallet balance

Once users had available balance, the act of investing became immediate. When they clicked to invest, the amount was automatically debited from the wallet.

3. We removed the transfer gap after reservation

This eliminated the fragile period between “investment selected” and “money actually received,” which had previously caused delays, drop-offs, and operational workarounds.

4. We routed repayments back into the wallet

When companies made installment payments, those amounts were redistributed back to investors through the same account structure.

5. We made idle balance economically useful

The wallet balance earned interest based on the Brazilian base rate at the time, giving users a reason to keep funds there rather than seeing the wallet as dead money.

6. We created a cleaner reinvestment loop

Because users already had money available in their account—and because repayments returned there automatically—they could reinvest much more easily in future loan offerings.

This changed the wallet from a settlement tool into a core part of the investment experience.

Outcome

The wallet model was highly successful from an operational standpoint and meaningfully improved the investor experience.

The clearest effects were:

10% waiting list recall rate reduced to 0
much less manual operational handling
fewer failures caused by delayed or missing transfers
a cleaner and more reliable allocation process
less dependence on waiting lists and follow-up calls
a faster, simpler experience for investors
a better foundation for repeat investing and portfolio growth

It also improved the system structurally. Instead of rebuilding certainty every week through manual coordination, we created a process in which investment actions were backed by available funds from the start.

Why this worked

This worked because it solved the problem at the right layer.

A weaker solution would have been:

more reminders
better instructions
countdown timers
more operational follow-up

Those might have improved symptoms, but not the system.

The wallet worked because it changed the structure of the journey:

it turned intent into funded action
it reduced reliance on external manual behavior
it simplified payout distribution
it created continuity between investing, earning, and reinvesting

In other words, it reduced friction not by pushing users harder, but by removing the broken handoff in the flow.

It also aligned the product much better with how investment platforms should ideally behave: money moves inside the system with clarity and predictability, instead of depending on scattered external steps.

What I learned

This case reinforced a lesson I value a lot: sometimes the best product improvement is not a new feature on the surface, but a better transaction architecture underneath it.

At first glance, this looked like an operations issue. But the real issue was that the product had separated selection from settlement in a way that created uncertainty for everyone involved.

By introducing a wallet, we solved an immediate operational problem, improved the investor experience, and unlocked a more strategic behavior loop through reinvestment. That made the solution more valuable than a simple process fix.

It also showed me how useful it is when product decisions are informed by operational exposure. Because I was close to the operations team, I could see pain points that might not have appeared clearly in analytics alone.

Case Name:
Funding Channel Sorting Hat

Case Narrative:
Funding Channel Sorting Hat
How we designed a transparent loan allocation system as Nexoos evolved from a peer-to-peer marketplace into a multi-channel lender

Short summary

As Nexoos evolved beyond a pure peer-to-peer lending marketplace and began raising institutional capital through debentures, we faced a new challenge: how to allocate approved loans fairly across two different funding channels. Some loans would be offered publicly through the marketplace to retail investors, while others would be allocated to private institutional portfolios. To protect trust, prepare for future regulatory maturity, and avoid biased selection, we created a rule-based allocation service internally nicknamed Sorting Hat. The service evaluated each approved loan against a defined set of characteristics—such as loan size, industry, risk profile, and concentration factors—and then assigned it programmatically to one of the two channels. This created a more auditable, balanced, and transparent allocation process, helping Nexoos scale its capital model without undermining marketplace fairness.

Context

Nexoos began as a peer-to-peer lending marketplace. Small and medium enterprises came to the platform seeking loans, while retail investors collectively funded those loans as an investment opportunity.

As the company evolved, the funding model became more sophisticated. In preparation for becoming a financial institution, Nexoos also began raising institutional capital through debentures. Those debenture structures were backed by portfolios of performing loans, creating a second funding channel alongside the public marketplace.

At that point, the company was no longer dealing with a single path for approved credit. It was dealing with two parallel funding channels:

a public marketplace channel for retail investors
a private institutional channel tied to debenture structures

That introduced a new strategic problem: once a loan was approved, how should the company decide where it went?

This was not a purely operational question. It touched portfolio construction, fairness, governance, and investor trust.

The problem

Once Nexoos began operating with both retail and institutional funding channels, loan allocation could no longer be treated informally.

Without a clear system, there was an obvious risk: people could reasonably question whether the “best” loans were being directed toward one channel over the other. Even if decisions were being made in good faith, the absence of an explicit and auditable mechanism would create room for doubt.

We needed a way to solve several problems at once:

Fairness: ensure that one channel was not systematically favored over the other
Portfolio balance: avoid over-concentration by industry, loan size, risk band, or borrower history
Transparency: make the allocation logic explainable internally and defensible externally
Auditability: create a record of how decisions were made
Scalability: replace ad hoc judgment with a repeatable system that could support a more mature financial institution model

The challenge was especially sensitive because the two channels had different visibility models. Marketplace loans were public to retail investors, while institutional allocations were private. That meant trust could not rely on visibility alone. It had to rely on the credibility of the allocation method itself.

Key insight

The key insight was that fairness in this context could not depend on good intentions or manual judgment alone. It had to be designed into the system.

We were not simply choosing a destination for each loan. We were effectively shaping two portfolios over time. That meant each new allocation had to be evaluated not only on the characteristics of the loan itself, but also on the current state of each channel’s portfolio.

In other words, the right question was not:

“Which channel should receive this loan?”

It was:

“Given the characteristics of this approved loan and the current composition of both portfolios, which allocation keeps the system more balanced, defensible, and fair over time?”

That shift in thinking mattered.

It turned the problem from a case-by-case placement exercise into a programmatic portfolio balancing problem.

And once we saw it that way, the solution became clearer: create a rule-based allocation service that could evaluate each loan consistently, apply explicit criteria, and log its decisions.

My role

I worked on shaping this allocation logic as Nexoos expanded beyond its original marketplace model into a more complex funding structure. The work required thinking across product, capital channels, trust, and operational governance.

This was not just about routing inventory. It was about designing a mechanism that the company could rely on internally and explain externally as it moved toward becoming a more mature financial institution.

I helped drive the framing of the problem as one of fairness, portfolio balance, and transparency—not just one of operational distribution—and contributed to the creation of a clear, rule-based system for allocation.

Constraints

This case came with real constraints, which is part of what makes it strong.

Two different capital channels had to coexist without undermining each other
Retail trust had to be protected, especially because public investors could reasonably worry about adverse selection
Institutional structures needed portfolio quality and concentration control
Allocation decisions had to be explainable and reviewable
Manual allocation could introduce bias, inconsistency, or suspicion even if unintentional
Portfolio balance could not be defined by a single variable such as credit risk alone

This last point is important. A balanced portfolio is multi-dimensional. It is not enough to say both channels contain similar average risk. They also need to be monitored for concentration by industry, borrower profile, loan size, historical performance, and related dimensions.

That made the problem meaningfully more sophisticated than a simple rules engine.

Solution

We created a service internally called Sorting Hat, a programmatic allocation layer that evaluated each approved loan and assigned it to the most appropriate funding channel based on explicit balancing logic.

1. We made allocation a formal system rather than an informal choice

Instead of relying on manual or opaque decision-making, each approved loan passed through a defined service before being assigned to either the retail marketplace or the institutional channel.

2. We evaluated each loan across multiple characteristics

The service checked factors such as:

loan size
industry
assessed credit risk
previous performance history
concentration and balance considerations across the current portfolios

This prevented the system from over-optimizing for only one dimension.

3. We treated allocation as a portfolio-balancing problem

Sorting Hat did not simply classify loans in isolation. It considered the state of both portfolios and aimed to keep them balanced over time, reducing concentration risk and avoiding systematic skew toward one channel.

4. We made the rules explicit

The criteria used for allocation were defined programmatically, which meant the logic was not hidden in personal judgment or informal operational practice.

5. We created logs and traceability

Because the process ran through a service, decisions were recorded and could be reviewed later. This was critical for auditability and for explaining how a particular allocation had happened.

6. We improved the basis for investor trust

Even though the institutional channel was private, the company could still demonstrate that allocation was being handled through consistent logic rather than subjective preference. That made the process more defensible to retail investors and more robust internally.

This was essentially a governance product: a tool that quietly shaped how fairness and balance worked underneath the business.

Outcome

Sorting Hat helped Nexoos operate a more mature multi-channel funding model without relying on opaque manual allocation.

The most important outcomes were:

more consistent allocation decisions
better balance across marketplace and institutional portfolios over time
reduced risk of bias or perceived bias
clearer internal governance around how approved loans were distributed
stronger auditability through explicit rules and logs
better ability to communicate fairness and discipline in the process

It also helped support a broader strategic transition. As Nexoos prepared to become a financial institution, this kind of programmatic control mattered. It showed that portfolio construction and channel allocation were being handled in a disciplined and transparent way, rather than through improvised judgment.

If you later remember measurable indicators, this section would become even stronger with metrics such as:

similarity of portfolio distributions across channels
reduction in concentration by sector or risk band
percentage of allocations handled automatically
reduction in manual exceptions
number of auditable decisions logged
improvement in investor confidence or complaint reduction

Even without precise numbers, the case is still strong because the value is structural.

Why this worked

This worked because it addressed the real source of the problem: trust in allocation logic.

A weaker solution would have been to keep allocating manually and simply document decisions afterward. But that would still leave too much room for inconsistency, bias, and suspicion.

Sorting Hat was stronger because it changed the system itself:

it made the rules visible
it applied them consistently
it considered portfolio effects over time
it produced a record that could be audited and explained

This is important: in a marketplace with asymmetric visibility, fairness is not achieved only by showing everything. Sometimes it is achieved by building a mechanism that is disciplined enough to deserve trust.

That is what this system did.

It also helped bridge two stages of company maturity:

the original marketplace logic
the more institution-like capital allocation discipline required for the next phase

So the solution worked not only because it solved today’s allocation problem, but because it prepared the company for the kind of governance it would need tomorrow.

What I learned

This case taught me that some product problems are really institution design problems.

At first glance, this could look like a routing tool. But the deeper challenge was designing a fair system for distributing opportunity across different capital channels under conditions of partial visibility.

What made the work meaningful was that we did not try to solve fairness through messaging alone. We solved it by encoding principles into the process itself.

It also reinforced a lesson I value a lot: when trust is fragile, transparency is not just about exposing outputs. It is also about making the mechanism behind those outputs coherent, reviewable, and defensible.

For me, this case sits at the intersection of product thinking, governance, and financial systems design—and that is exactly why it remains one of the most interesting ones.

Case Name:
Smartpricing

Case Narrative:
From Saturation to Recovery: Designing a Smart Pricing System for Stranded Listings
How we used timing, communication, and automated price adjustment to restore liquidity in QuintoAndar’s rental marketplace

Short summary

At QuintoAndar, I worked on marketplace liquidity, with a particular focus on “stranded listings”: properties that stayed live for too long without being rented. We knew that listing performance saturated quickly—if a property did not rent within the first 6–8 weeks, its chances of renting later dropped sharply. Since price was the most scalable lever available, we experimented with a smart pricing system that could automatically suggest or apply price reductions over time. Early versions failed because owners were asked to opt in before they felt the pain of low demand, and because price intervention happened too early, when confidence in the original asking price was still high. The solution that worked was a later, progressive intervention model: owners were default-enrolled, warned well in advance, and only received automated price changes once poor performance had become clear. This helped revive stale listings, improved their marketplace visibility, and extended conversion beyond the point where the curve would normally flatten.

Context

When I joined QuintoAndar as a Product Manager, I was working on liquidity in the rental marketplace. One of the most important supply-side problems we faced was the existence of stranded listings: properties that remained published for a long time without successfully getting rented.

This mattered because marketplace supply is not equally healthy over time. A newly listed property has freshness, ranking momentum, and a good chance of attracting attention. But if it remains available too long, performance starts to deteriorate. It receives less qualified demand, owners become frustrated, and the listing begins to act like dead weight inside the marketplace.

We had other levers to improve listing performance, such as better photos or operational interventions, but those were less systematic and harder to scale. Price, by contrast, was the strongest and most scalable lever we could use across the marketplace.

That made smart pricing a natural place to intervene.

The problem

The problem was not simply that some listings were overpriced. The deeper issue was that once a listing underperformed for long enough, it entered a negative spiral.

A stranded listing typically suffered from several reinforcing dynamics:

it had already missed the period of highest freshness and attention
owners were often anchored to their initial pricing expectations
the market had already signaled weak demand
the ranking system would tend to favor stronger-performing supply
after a certain point, the chance of renting dropped sharply

We observed that listing conversion saturated quickly. If a property had not rented within roughly two months, very few would rent afterward without intervention.

In theory, price was the right lever. In practice, however, price is emotionally sensitive. Owners tend to believe their property deserves more, especially early on. So the challenge was not just to lower prices. It was to determine when and how price intervention should happen so that owners would accept it and the marketplace would benefit from it.

Key insight

The key insight was that timing mattered more than the pricing mechanism itself.

Our first iterations assumed that owners would rationally agree upfront to future automated price adjustments if it helped their property rent faster. That turned out to be wrong.

When owners were still early in the journey, they had not yet felt the pain of poor marketplace performance. At that stage, many believed their property had the right price and just needed more time. So asking them to opt into future price reductions during registration felt abstract and unattractive.

We also learned that intervening too early created regret. Even among owners who had opted in, early price reductions often triggered pushback:

“we should have waited longer”
“my property has special characteristics”
“the market hasn’t had enough time yet”
“I don’t agree with lowering it already”

So the real insight was not “automate pricing.”
It was:

Automated pricing only works once the owner can clearly see that the original price is not performing.

That shifted the problem from a feature design problem to a behavioral timing and communication problem.

My role

As Product Manager working on marketplace liquidity, I focused on how to reduce stranded inventory using scalable levers. In this case, that meant helping shape the smart pricing strategy across multiple iterations: identifying why the early version underperformed, reframing the problem around owner timing and acceptance, and driving a solution that better aligned automation with marketplace signals and user psychology.

This was not just about enabling price changes. It was about finding a product mechanism that owners would accept and that would genuinely improve supply-side performance at scale.

Constraints

This case had several important constraints:

price is the strongest liquidity lever, but also one of the most emotionally sensitive
owners are naturally anchored to their original valuation
early intervention can feel unfair or premature
late intervention may come after the listing has already lost too much momentum
communications had to preserve trust, even when the system was recommending lower prices
the feature had to be systematic and scalable, not dependent on manual ops handling
the marketplace ranking system interacted with price, so the effect of pricing changes was partly indirect as well as direct

That last point is especially important. A lower price did not just change affordability. It also changed how the listing behaved inside the marketplace ecosystem, including ranking and visibility.

So the product was really influencing both:

user conversion potential
marketplace distribution dynamics
Solution

We went through multiple iterations, and that evolution is part of what makes the case compelling.

1. First iteration: optional enrollment during listing registration

Initially, smart pricing was presented during the onboarding or registration flow. Owners could choose to enable a mode in which QuintoAndar would suggest new prices if the property failed to attract enough demand after the first month.

The logic seemed reasonable, but adoption was weak.

Owners had not yet experienced low performance, so they did not see much value in the feature. Most believed they were already pricing correctly and had little motivation to pre-authorize future price reductions.

2. Early intervention also created resistance

For the smaller number of owners who did enable the feature, early price movement often backfired. Lowering the price too soon made people feel that the system was acting before the market had given their listing a fair chance.

That led to frustration and weakened trust in the mechanism.

3. We shifted from opt-in logic to default-enabled logic with opt-out

A key change was making smart pricing effectively on by default during registration, while still allowing owners to opt out.

This made the mechanism more scalable, but by itself it was not enough. The real improvement came from changing when the feature activated and how it was communicated.

4. We delayed intervention until poor performance was clearly visible

Rather than adjusting pricing early, we only activated the pricing intervention later in the listing lifecycle—around week 6 or week 8, once it had become clear that the property was underperforming.

At that point, the owner was much more likely to recognize the problem and be open to corrective action.

5. We introduced progressive communication well before any change

This was a critical part of the successful version.

Instead of surprising owners with an automatic change, we started communicating in advance that the property’s performance was below expectations and that a pricing adjustment would happen if results did not improve.

The communication cadence included reminders such as:

around 10 days before
7 days before
3 days before
1 day before

The tone also mattered. The system reminded users:

smart pricing was enabled
the listing was underperforming
price changes would occur only if performance did not improve
they could still turn the feature off at any time

This gave owners time to emotionally process the situation and understand the rationale.

6. We used price reductions to reactivate marketplace performance

Once prices began moving downward in the successful iteration, those listings became more competitive and also benefited from marketplace re-ranking. In effect, the pricing change gave stale listings a second life.

So the mechanism worked in two ways:

it improved the attractiveness of the property to demand
it increased visibility by helping the listing perform better inside the ranking system
Outcome

enter a mandate-style agreement with the owner

effectively secure the listing within our own channel
then re-rent it to the end tenant

That changed the economics completely.

The key insight was that the model’s main risk was not operational complexity or even vacancy alone. It was adverse selection driven by pricing asymmetry.

In simple terms:

when we priced a property too low, owners were likely to reject the offer because they believed the market would pay more
when we priced a property too high, owners were much more likely to accept—precisely because the offer was better than what the market would likely deliver

That created a dangerous selection dynamic:
the deals most likely to enter the portfolio were disproportionately the ones where QuintoAndar had overestimated value.

So the challenge was not just “can we price properties?”
It was:

Can we price them accurately enough that the accepted subset of properties still produces a healthy portfolio outcome?

we validated the concept under constrained conditions
identified the structural limit
and chose not to expand beyond the model’s actual edge

That is good product work.

The market did not correct our errors evenly. Owners filtered them for us:

underpriced offers were rejected
overpriced offers were accepted

That meant the portfolio was biased toward the mistakes that hurt us most.

2. Pricing quality was too geography-dependent

In areas where QuintoAndar had dense historical data, pricing could be accurate enough to make the model viable. In less mature regions, the model became far riskier.

strong strategic rationale
clear marketplace pain

Short summary

As Senior Head of Product, I led a search thesis across QuintoAndar’s rental and for-sale marketplaces focused on a core tension: the more we ask users about what they want, the more friction we create—but if we ask too little, search becomes too broad to produce relevant results. From there, we redesigned the search experience to guide users more naturally toward qualification by unifying location and basic filters into a single flow, hiding overly specific options unless requested, and reducing UI confusion between search and filtering. Context

By the time I was leading this work, search had become one of the most important cross-marketplace surfaces at QuintoAndar. It mattered in both rentals and sales, but not in exactly the same way.

In both businesses, search was the gateway to discovery. It shaped:

what people saw first
how quickly they found relevant inventory
how much they trusted the marketplace
and how efficiently they moved toward a visit or transaction

But the two marketplaces had different structural dynamics.

In rentals:

supply was tighter
demand was relatively stronger
users could often find relevant options with less exploration

In sales:

inventory density was much higher
there were many more listings to sift through
buyers had more specific, higher-stakes preferences
and search precision mattered much more for relevance

That meant search was not just a utility surface. It was a major lever for marketplace performance, especially in sales.

The problem

The core problem was a classic product tension:

If we ask users for too little, search is too vague to be useful. If we ask for too much, search becomes too heavy to start.

At one extreme, a user could search something very broad—say, all of São Paulo with no meaningful filters. In that case, the system had very little signal about what would actually be relevant.

At the other extreme, we could try to force qualification upfront through a long questionnaire. But that would create so much friction that many users would abandon the experience before even seeing results.

So the challenge was not simply “improve search.”
It was:

How do we guide users toward giving us enough signal to produce good results, while keeping the experience light enough to preserve exploration?

Once we started digging into the data and user behavior, we saw that many searches were poorly qualified:

location scope was too broad
basic filters were missing
the experience made it harder than it should have to combine location and filtering coherently

This was hurting both relevance and trust.

Key insight

There were two big insights in this case.

Insight 1: not all searches are equally meaningful

We needed a clear internal definition of what a good search looked like.

I defined the concept of a qualified search as one that had:

a sufficiently precise location, such as neighborhood, street, building/condo, or point-of-interest-based area
plus a basic set of filters:
property type (house/apartment)
price range
number of rooms

This was important because it turned search qualification into something measurable and actionable. Instead of vaguely saying “users should search better,” we could now track and improve the share of searches that carried enough signal to support good ranking and retrieval.

search bar = location only
filter button = everything else (and it had a "search filters" bar inside it)

users typed neighborhoods into the filter search field
users expected to be able to express more of their intent in the main search entry point
the existence of multiple search-like fields broke the flow

So the problem was not only insufficient qualification.
It was that the product’s structure made qualification harder than it needed to be.

So when leadership aligned around moving ahead, the real project became:

Combine conversational search and visual retrieval into a new search experience that still respects the hard lessons we had already learned about qualification and trust.

That is the true context.

The problem

The problem was not “users need a chatbot.”

The problem was that traditional real-estate search still had important expressive limitations.

Even with a large set of structured filters, users often wanted to search for things that did not map cleanly to the filter model:

visual traits
lifestyle-oriented cues
qualitative property characteristics
natural-language phrasing that did not fit the product taxonomy

At the same time, we already knew from previous work that qualified search mattered. If people searched too broadly, results degraded. So we could not simply introduce a free-form chat box and let search become vague again.

That created a layered challenge:

1. Let users express richer intent

Users needed a more natural way to say what they wanted.

2. Preserve qualification

The system still had to gather the minimum amount of structured signal needed for good search performance.

3. Build trust quickly

Users were already skeptical of chatbots, and any sign of irrelevance would cause them to lose confidence immediately.

4. Make the experience legible

People had to understand that they could now search for things they had never been able to search for before.

So the problem was much broader than adding a new input method. It was:

How do you introduce LLM conversation into a marketplace search experience without losing the precision, trust, and retrieval quality that search already depends on?

That is the real product problem.

Key insight

There were three major insights in this case.

Insight 1: conversational search only works if it still qualifies intent

We already knew from earlier search work that good search results depend on good user signal. So introducing an LLM could not mean abandoning qualification. It had to mean making qualification feel more natural.

That was a very important distinction.

The conversational agent had to understand when the user had not yet provided enough structure and gently guide them toward a qualified search. In practice, that meant making sure a search included:

non-city-level location
price range
room count
property type

So the LLM was not replacing the qualification thesis. It was a new interface for it.

Insight 2: in AI search, trust is established visually and immediately

We found that users were skeptical. They were ready to doubt the system quickly. So trust had to be earned in the first seconds.

The most important trust signal was not the text of the conversation. It was the visible result.

If users asked for something visually specific, they had to see evidence of that immediately. For example:

if they searched for greenery outside the window, that should show up right away
if they searched for wooden floors, the returned photos should surface that visually

This insight forced us to rethink ranking much more deeply than a typical chatbot wrapper would.

Insight 3: multimodal search changes the meaning of a listing result

In traditional search, the listing card is a relatively stable object. In this product, the listing card became partly query-dependent.

A listing was no longer just:

the same ranking
the same cover photo
the same representation every time

Instead, when the search contained visual intent, the system had to:

rank based on visual similarity
choose the most relevant photo
and make that photo the cover for that specific query

That was a profound shift. It changed not only how search worked, but how inventory was represented.

My role

As Senior Head of Product, I pushed for search to become QuintoAndar’s first serious LLM product and led the thesis from concept through proof of concept, alpha, and beta.

My role included:

arguing for search as the first AI use case
connecting the earlier search qualification thesis with the new conversational model
shaping the product direction across text, voice, and visual search
aligning the UI, ranking, and trust strategy
navigating trade-offs with data science and other stakeholders around ranking architecture
and using the initiative not only as a feature launch, but as a way to build the company’s first real AI product muscle

This was not just an experimentation case. It was also a strategic-entry case: how to introduce a new technology into a serious marketplace without turning it into gimmickry.

Constraints

This case had unusually interesting constraints.

Technology constraints
LLMs were still immature and error-prone
users were skeptical of chatbots
free-form input created ambiguity
visual similarity models produced probabilistic rather than binary outputs
Search constraints
qualification still mattered
the system had an existing ranking stack that had already been carefully built
exact relevance for location and structured filters could not be sacrificed
Organizational constraints
this was one of the first true LLM initiatives in the company
there was little existing product muscle around AI
data science and search logic already had strong established models
introducing a new ranking path was naturally controversial
UX constraints
users had to understand what was new
they had to see when the system was “doing something different”
the experience had to avoid feeling like a generic chatbot bolted onto listings
confidence signals had to be designed carefully, especially when visual matches were not binary

These constraints are a big part of why the case is good. It was not an AI demo. It was an actual product problem.

Solution

The product evolved through several stages:

proof of concept in roughly 2 months
alpha around 4 months
beta around 9 months, when we launched the A/B test

The solution combined several layers.

1. We introduced a conversational entry point into search

Inside the existing search qualification area, we added an AI entry point that made the new capability visible and legible. Users could interact with it by:

typing
or voice

The system would take the conversation and translate it into a search query in the backend.

This created a more natural way to express intent, especially for needs that were awkward to represent in the traditional filter model.

2. We preserved qualification through the agent itself

The conversational agent did not simply accept vague input and return whatever it could.

If the user had not provided a qualified search, the agent would actively prompt for the basics:

price range
number of rooms
property type
and sufficiently specific location

This was one of the most important design decisions in the whole product. It let us maintain the strengths of structured search without forcing people through a traditional filter form.

3. We expanded retrieval beyond structured filters into photos

This was the real wow layer.

The system could now search for aspects that had previously been invisible to the product’s structured search model. Instead of only using the standard set of real-estate filters, we could also retrieve based on visual and descriptive characteristics extracted from photos.

This meant users could ask for things that lived more naturally in human language and visual perception.

4. We changed ranking behavior for visually grounded queries

A major challenge was that the existing ranking stack had been built for traditional search, and it was already mature.

But for conversational and visual-intent queries, ranking had to behave differently. If the user asked for something visual, the best match needed to appear right away or trust would disappear.

So we created a compromise:

for queries with meaningful visual intent, we used vector-distance-based visual ranking
for queries that stayed within traditional structured intent, even if they came through the conversational interface, we preserved the original ranking stack

This was a smart mid-term solution because it let us innovate without discarding everything the existing ranking system already did well.

5. We made the most relevant photo become the listing cover

This was one of the boldest product changes.

Traditionally, the cover photo of a listing was static and chosen by the photographer. In the new system, when visual intent was present, the most relevant photo for that query became the cover.

That meant:

the best-matching photo surfaced first
the listing itself became more query-responsive
the user immediately saw proof that the system had understood the request

This was critical for trust.

6. We introduced confidence-sensitive UI behavior

Traditional filters are binary: the listing either has the feature or it does not.

Visual matching is not binary. It gives you scores and degrees of similarity.

So we had to make product decisions about confidence:

when do we explicitly claim the listing has what the user asked for?
when do we stay quieter and just rank it higher without making a strong promise?

A good example was the “has everything you asked for” style of message. We only showed that when confidence was very high. Otherwise, we avoided overstating what the system knew.

That was an important design principle:
never let the UI sound more certain than the model actually is.

Outcome

The product was successful across the three fronts that mattered most.

1. Business results

The conversion gains were more modest than in some of your previous search work, but still positive. The strongest effect appeared in sale, where buyers had more specific intent and more inventory to evaluate. That made the ability to search through photos and natural language more valuable.

2. AI muscle development

This was QuintoAndar’s first meaningful LLM product effort. The work helped the company develop:

practical product muscle around LLMs
an early internal AI platform
experience operating an AI product in a real marketplace context

That is strategically important and absolutely worth highlighting.

3. Marketable innovation positioning

The product also worked as a strong narrative asset for the company. It was novel, demonstrable, and visually impressive in a way that many AI projects are not. That gave QuintoAndar a credible story about innovation in the market.

This three-part success is one of the best aspects of the case. Even if the raw conversion gain was not massive, the project still clearly paid off strategically.

Why this worked

This worked because it avoided the two common failure modes of AI product launches.

Failure mode 1: treating AI as a wrapper

A lot of teams add a chatbot to an existing experience without rethinking ranking, retrieval, or trust. That usually produces novelty without real utility.

You did not do that.

Failure mode 2: treating AI as magic

Another common mistake is to assume that because users can type freely, the system no longer needs structure, guidance, or quality controls.

You did not do that either.

Instead, this worked because it combined:

1. New interaction with old lessons

The conversational agent still enforced the core search lesson: qualification matters.

2. AI novelty with trust-first execution

You recognized that trust would come from immediate visual proof, not from clever copy.

3. Model capability with product restraint

You did not overclaim confidence. The UI reflected uncertainty carefully.

4. Innovation with strategic pragmatism

You did not replace the whole ranking stack. You selectively bypassed it only when the query type truly required it.

5. Business value with broader organizational value

The project was designed not only to lift conversion, but also to create AI capability and marketable positioning.

That is a sophisticated definition of success.

What I learned

This case taught me that AI products become powerful when they are treated as new operating layers, not just new surfaces.

The conversational interface mattered, but it was not enough on its own. To make the product genuinely useful, we had to rethink:

qualification
ranking
visual evidence
confidence communication
and how a result card itself should behave

The biggest lesson for me was:

In AI search, trust is won less by language and more by retrieval quality.

If the first result proves the system understood the request, users will forgive a lot. If it does not, they will distrust the whole experience immediately.

It also reinforced that early AI work should often be judged on more than one axis. In emerging technology, a project can be worth doing because it delivers:

some business impact
meaningful capability development
and strategic brand value at the same time

That was exactly the case here.
